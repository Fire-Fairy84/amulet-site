Bastos

Three of Wands

What you launched is actually moving. First returns arriving; partnerships forming; the wait that is active, not idle. The field turned out wider than the plan assumed — adjust upward.

A figure stands on a cliff, back to the viewer, watching three ships cross a golden sea. Three wands are planted around them, upright and steady — decisions already made, holding their ground. The figure's hand rests on one of them. Nothing here is beginning. Everything here is in transit.

expansion, ventures underway, foresight rewarded, ships in motion, the active wait, the wider field opening

Upright

The ships are out. That is the difference between this card and every planning card before it: something real left the harbor with your name on it — the application, the launch, the offer, the work released to strangers — and is now crossing water you do not control. The Three of Wands is the strange season after commitment and before results, and its first message is simple: this is a real place, and you are standing in it correctly.

Notice the posture. Back to us, hand on a planted wand, facing the sea. Not pacing the dock, not rowing out to check on the cargo, not drafting the ship a new route mid-crossing. The figure has done what could be done and now does the other work — the one nobody praises — of letting the sent thing travel. There is a competence in this kind of waiting. It is not passivity; the wands are planted, the watch is kept, the harbor is ready. But the pull to interfere, to follow up too soon, to unsay and resay — the card holds a steady hand against all of it.

Foresight is being rewarded, which is worth pausing on: the position you hold now exists because of decisions made seasons ago, when they were harder and less obvious. Whatever arrives on these ships, the vantage itself is already the proof that planting works — that the version of you who committed back then read the horizon right. Bank that. It is evidence you will want during the next planting season, when the sea is empty again.

And the sea is golden, wider than the harbor map suggested. Mid-venture is when the actual size of the field reveals itself — the second market, the unexpected reply, the door behind the door. The card's counsel: let the venture be as big as it is turning out to be. Plans made from the shore were sized by shore imagination. You are not on the shore anymore.

At work

First returns, partnerships forming, the launched thing gaining water. Hold the follow-up urge to a schedule; prepare the harbor for what returns.

In love

The reach beyond the familiar circle — the invitation accepted, the distance closed by something you actually sent. In transit is a real status; honor it.

In you

Patience with things in motion. What you set in motion months ago is working — let the evidence arrive before revising the self-assessment.

Note: Not arrival — the ships are still out. But not stalling either: transit is the venture working, at sea speed.

Reversed

The launch that met headwinds. Effort out, nothing back yet — or the reach that genuinely exceeded the grasp. Obstacles between you and the return; discouragement mid-crossing. Delay, which is not the same word as shipwreck.

The ships are late. Not sunk — late, and the card is strict about keeping those words separate, because the mind under pressure merges them. Something you sent out has not come back on schedule: the replies, the sales, the results, the response the effort seemed to promise. The reversed Three is the sour middle of a venture, where the sending is long past and the receiving refuses to start, and the horizon-scanning has become a tic rather than a watch.

First, run the honest split: how much of this is sea, and how much was ship? Some delay is weather — markets, timing, other people's queues — and no amount of cliff-standing alters it. But some ventures were loaded wrong at the dock: the reach set past the grasp, the offer priced by hope, the plan sized for a version of the field that the field declined to be. The reversed card does not say which is yours. It says the split can be computed now, with information the launch day never had, and that computing it beats scanning.

Watch what the waiting is doing to you, because that is the live damage. Effort that receives no echo starts to eat its own sender: the daily horizon check that returns nothing, returns something — a small verdict, false but cumulative. Discouragement mid-crossing has convinced better sailors than you that the sea is permanent. The wands leaning behind you were planted by someone with reasons; the reasons have not expired because the wind is slow.

What the card asks for is not more patience, exactly — it is redirected activity. The harbor can be readied; the next cargo can be built; one ship's route can be honestly re-examined and, if truly misdrawn, written off with ceremony rather than watched into legend. Overdue is a season with three exits: the late arrival, the corrected relaunch, the honest loss. All three are business. Only the fourth thing — the years of scanning — is not.

At work

The deal stuck in someone else's harbor; the launch that met headwinds. Split the delay honestly: weather, or loading error. Then work the harbor, not the horizon.

In love

Effort out, nothing back yet. The reach toward someone that is still in transit — or was over-sized for what the field could return. Give it a date, not an eternity.

In you

Discouragement mid-crossing, mistaking slow wind for verdict. The daily empty-horizon check, quietly eating the sender.

Note: Not shipwreck — delay. The words are different, and the difference is the whole card.
